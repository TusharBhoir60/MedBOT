# Anemia

## Symptoms
- Fatigue and weakness
- Pallor (pale skin, conjunctival pallor, nail bed pallor)
- Shortness of breath on exertion
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat (tachycardia, palpitations)
- Cold hands and feet
- Brittle nails (koilonychia in iron deficiency)
- Headache
- Pica (craving non-food items such as ice or clay)

## Risk Factors
- Iron-deficient diet (vegetarian without supplementation)
- Heavy menstrual periods (menorrhagia)
- Pregnancy
- Chronic diseases (kidney disease, cancer, inflammatory conditions)
- Gastrointestinal blood loss (ulcers, colon polyps)
- Malabsorption syndromes (coeliac disease, Crohn disease)
- Sickle cell disease or thalassemia (genetic)
- Age >65 years
- Hookworm infection (endemic regions)

## Red Flags
- Haemoglobin <7 g/dL (severe anaemia)
- Tachycardia at rest (HR >100 bpm)
- Syncope or pre-syncope
- Chest pain or signs of heart failure
- Melena or haematochezia (GI bleeding)
- Jaundice with anaemia (haemolytic anaemia)
- Petechiae or purpura (pancytopenia concern)
- Rapid-onset pallor with dyspnoea

## Differential Diagnosis
- Iron deficiency anaemia (microcytic, low ferritin)
- Vitamin B12 / folate deficiency (megaloblastic, macrocytic)
- Anaemia of chronic disease (normocytic, elevated ferritin)
- Haemolytic anaemia (elevated LDH, reticulocyte count, indirect bilirubin)
- Aplastic anaemia (pancytopenia, hypocellular marrow)
- Thalassemia (target cells, family history)
- Sickle cell disease (pain crises, sickle cells on smear)

## Urgency
- **Routine**: Mild anaemia (Hb 10–12 g/dL) — dietary counselling, oral iron/B12 supplementation, follow-up in 4–6 weeks.
- **Urgent**: Moderate anaemia (Hb 7–10 g/dL) with symptoms — investigate underlying cause, consider parenteral iron.
- **Emergency**: Severe anaemia (Hb <7 g/dL) or haemodynamic instability — blood transfusion, urgent haematology referral.

## Source
WHO Haemoglobin Concentrations for Anaemia Diagnosis (2011), NICE Anaemia Guidelines (NG150).
