# Malaria

## Symptoms
- Periodic or cyclical high fever with chills and rigors
- Profuse sweating following fever episodes
- Headache
- Muscle aches and fatigue
- Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea
- Anaemia (pallor, weakness)
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes)
- Enlarged spleen (splenomegaly)

## Risk Factors
- Residence in or travel to endemic regions (sub-Saharan Africa, South/Southeast Asia, Central America)
- Lack of prophylaxis medication
- Exposure to Anopheles mosquito bites (dusk to dawn)
- Young children (<5 years) and pregnant women
- Immunocompromised patients
- Sickle cell trait (provides partial protection)

## Red Flags
- Cerebral malaria: altered consciousness, seizures, coma
- Severe anaemia (haemoglobin <5 g/dL)
- Respiratory distress / pulmonary oedema
- Hypoglycaemia
- Renal failure (dark urine, oliguria)
- Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
- Parasitaemia >5% on blood smear

## Differential Diagnosis
- Dengue fever (similar endemic regions, rash more common)
- Typhoid fever (sustained fever, rose spots)
- Viral hepatitis (jaundice, elevated liver enzymes)
- Leptospirosis (occupational exposure)
- Meningitis (neck stiffness, photophobia)
- Sepsis (general systemic infection markers)

## Urgency
- **Routine**: Uncomplicated P. vivax or P. falciparum malaria — oral antimalarials (ACT), outpatient monitoring.
- **Urgent**: Recurrent episodes or treatment failure — reassess drug resistance, consider hospitalisation.
- **Emergency**: Severe/complicated malaria (cerebral malaria, severe anaemia, renal failure) — IV artesunate, ICU admission, blood transfusion if needed.

## Source
WHO Guidelines for the Treatment of Malaria, 3rd Edition (2015).
