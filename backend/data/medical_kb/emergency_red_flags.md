# Emergency Red Flags

## Symptoms
- Sudden severe chest pain (possible MI, PE, aortic dissection)
- Sudden severe headache (thunderclap — subarachnoid haemorrhage)
- Difficulty breathing or stridor
- Altered consciousness or unresponsiveness
- Seizures (new-onset or prolonged)
- Severe abdominal pain with rigidity (peritonitis, ruptured viscus)
- High fever (>40°C) with rash and neck stiffness (meningococcal sepsis)
- Profuse bleeding that cannot be controlled
- Signs of anaphylaxis (swelling, urticaria, hypotension, wheezing)
- Acute limb weakness or speech difficulty (stroke)

## Risk Factors
- Pre-existing cardiovascular disease
- Known coagulopathies or anticoagulant use
- Immunosuppression
- Recent surgery or trauma
- Pregnancy (ectopic pregnancy, eclampsia)
- Known allergies (insect stings, medications, foods)
- Extremes of age (neonates, elderly >75)
- Uncontrolled diabetes (DKA, hypoglycaemia)

## Red Flags
- Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) <12
- Systolic BP <90 mmHg (shock)
- Heart rate >130 bpm or <40 bpm
- Respiratory rate >30 or SpO2 <92%
- Temperature >40°C or <35°C
- Unilateral pupil dilation (raised ICP)
- Cyanosis
- Absent peripheral pulses

## Differential Diagnosis
- Myocardial infarction vs pulmonary embolism vs aortic dissection
- Stroke vs hypoglycaemia vs seizure (post-ictal state)
- Meningitis vs encephalitis vs febrile convulsion
- Anaphylaxis vs asthma exacerbation
- Ectopic pregnancy vs ovarian torsion vs appendicitis
- Diabetic ketoacidosis vs sepsis

## Urgency
- **Emergency**: ALL conditions listed in this document require immediate emergency intervention.
- Activate emergency medical services.
- Initiate ABCDE (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure) assessment.
- Do NOT delay transport for investigation.
