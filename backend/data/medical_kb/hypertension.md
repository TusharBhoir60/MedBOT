# Hypertension

## Symptoms
- Often asymptomatic (silent killer)
- Headache (especially occipital, morning headaches)
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Visual disturbances (blurred vision)
- Chest pain or tightness
- Shortness of breath
- Nosebleeds (epistaxis) — in severe cases
- Fatigue

## Risk Factors
- Age >45 years (men) or >55 years (women)
- Family history of hypertension
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- High sodium diet
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Chronic stress
- Smoking
- Diabetes mellitus
- Chronic kidney disease

## Red Flags
- Blood pressure >180/120 mmHg (hypertensive crisis)
- Sudden severe headache with altered consciousness
- Chest pain with dyspnoea (aortic dissection risk)
- Visual loss or papilloedema
- Seizures or focal neurological deficits (hypertensive encephalopathy)
- Haematuria or acute kidney injury
- Eclampsia in pregnant patients

## Differential Diagnosis
- Secondary hypertension (renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma, Cushing syndrome, hyperaldosteronism)
- White-coat hypertension
- Anxiety/panic disorder (episodic BP elevation)
- Hyperthyroidism (tachycardia, weight loss)
- Obstructive sleep apnoea

## Urgency
- **Routine**: Stage 1 hypertension (130–139/80–89 mmHg) — lifestyle modification, consider monotherapy.
- **Urgent**: Stage 2 hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg) — dual therapy initiation, follow-up within 1 month.
- **Emergency**: Hypertensive emergency (>180/120 mmHg with end-organ damage) — immediate IV antihypertensives, ICU monitoring.

## Source
ACC/AHA Hypertension Guidelines (2017), JNC 8 Recommendations.
